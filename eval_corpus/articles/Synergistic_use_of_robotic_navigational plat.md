Background:  Cavitary lung lesions represent a diagnostic challenge due to their heterogeneous etiology, 
which includes infectious, inflammatory, and malignant processes. Establishing an accurate diagnosis is 
essential, yet conventional techniques such as computed tomography-guided transthoracic biopsy, surgical 
biopsy and standard bronchoscopy may be limited by complication risk or suboptimal yield. Recent advances 
in robotic and ultrathin bronchoscopy have improved precision and reach, but their combined application 
remains underexplored.
Case Description:  We conducted a retrospective case series of six consecutive patients with cavitary 
lung lesions evaluated at a tertiary academic medical center. All procedures were performed by a single 
interventional pulmonologist using a standardized workflow. Pre-procedural lesion marking and airway 
mapping were completed in all cases with Intuitive PointPlan software to generate a virtual navigation 
roadmap. Ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy (Vathin disposable bronchoscope; 3.2-mm outer diameter, 
1.2-mm working channel) was used in all patients to access and sample the target cavities. In four cases 
performed under general anesthesia, the IonTM robotic bronchoscopy platform was additionally employed 
for navigational guidance and fluoroscopy-assisted biopsy, followed by ultrathin bronchoscopy to allow 
direct intracavitary visualization and targeted sampling. Successful navigation to the cavitary lesion was 
achieved in all six procedures. The combined robotic-ultrathin bronchoscopy approach provided additional 
diagnostic information beyond robotic-guided sampling alone in two of four cases. No procedure-related 
pneumothorax or significant bleeding occurred. One patient developed a transient post-procedural fever that 
resolved with antibiotic therapy. At 3-month follow-up, all patients demonstrated radiologic or symptomatic 
improvement consistent with the final diagnosis.
Conclusions:  The combined use of navigational robotic and ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy is a feasible, 
safe, and potentially superior approach for evaluating cavitary lung lesions. This workflow integrates precise 
navigation with direct intracavitary visualization, optimizing diagnostic yield while minimizing procedural 
risk.

As defined by the Fleischner Society, lung cavities 
constitute a gas-filled space within a nodule, mass, or area 
of consolidation, characterized by a wall thickness greater 
than 4 mm (1). Its differential diagnosis is broad, spanning 
infections, inflammatory conditions, malignancies, among 
others. Common infectious etiologies include Staphylococcus 
aureus  and Mycobacterium tuberculosis , while fungal and 
parasitic organisms are also implicated. Inflammatory 
causes include granulomatosis with polyangiitis, rheumatoid 
arthritis, and sarcoidosis. Both primary lung cancers, 
particularly squamous cell carcinoma, and metastatic 
malignancies can present with cavitary morphology (1,2).
Cavitary lung lesions represent a distinct diagnostic 
challenge because of their heterogeneous presentations 
and diverse causes. Given the broad differential diagnosis, 
establishing an accurate diagnosis is essential (2). Broadly, there are three primary approaches to biopsy these 
lesions, they include bronchoscopic techniques, computed 
tomography-guided transthoracic biopsy (CTTB), and 
surgical approach. Within the bronchoscopic field, both 
flexible and robotic navigational platforms have been used 
successfully to access and sample these lesions with favorable 
safety profiles. However, the combined use of ultrathin 
flexible bronchoscopy and navigational guidance remains 
underexplored, despite its potential to enhance diagnostic 
yield while maintaining procedural safety and simplicity.
In this manuscript we describe a workflow that integrates 
robotic and ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy within a single 
procedural session, designed to optimize diagnostic yield 
in cavitary lung lesions. The rationale for this approach 
lies in leveraging the roadmap created by navigational 
platforms to access the lesion and then employing ultrathin 
bronchoscopy to visualize the cavity directly and obtain 
targeted biopsies. By doing so, the operator benefits from 
both precise trajectory guidance and direct visualization, 
thereby overcoming limitations of each modality in isolation 
with a minimalistic tool footprint. This article is presented 
in accordance with the AME Case Series reporting 
checklist (available at https://jtd.amegroups.com/article/
view/10.21037/jtd-2025-aw-2052/rc ).
Case presentation
A retrospective case series was assembled including six 
consecutive patients with cavitary lung lesions requiring 
tissue and microbiological diagnosis. This case series 
was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of 
Helsinki and its subsequent amendments. The case series 
was approved by the University of Southern California 
Institutional Review Board (IRB) (No. HS-25-00578). 
Publication of this case series, accompanying images, and 
video was waived from patients’/participants’ consent 
according to the ethics committee due to the retrospective 
nature of this case series and the use of de-identified data. 
All procedures were performed within a 1-month period, 
encompassing both inpatient and outpatient referrals to 
the Interventional Pulmonology Service at Los Angeles 
General Medical Center, a tertiary academic institution 
and the largest county hospital in the city. All cases were 
performed by the same interventional pulmonology 
attending physician. Inclusion criteria were clearly defined: 
patients aged 18 years or older were eligible for inclusion, 
while pregnant patients were excluded. All procedures were

Key findings 
• This study presents a novel bronchoscopic workflow combining 
robotic navigation system and ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy 
to optimize the diagnostic yield of cavitary lung lesions. Using 
a robotic platform software to create a navigation pathway, an 
ultrathin bronchoscope was successfully guided into the target 
cavity in all six cases, enabling direct intracavitary visualization 
and precise tissue sampling. Furthermore, ultrathin bronchoscopy 
enhanced the diagnostic yield after standard biopsy procedure with 
robotic platform in half of the combined cases. No pneumothorax, 
bleeding, or major complications occurred, and all patients 
demonstrated radiological improvement at follow-up.  
What is known and what is new? 
• Cavitary lung lesions represent a diagnostic challenge due to their 
complex structure and broad differential diagnosis. T raditional 
modalities such as computed tomography-guided, surgical biopsy 
and standard bronchoscopy have limitations due to procedural risk 
and suboptimal diagnostic yield. 
• This study introduces a synergistic workflow that combines 
navigational precision from robotic software guidance with 
direct intracavitary visualization via ultrathin bronchoscopy. 
This integrated approach enhances diagnostic accuracy while 
maintaining procedural safety and efficiency.
What is the implication, and what should change now?
• The combined use of robotic and ultrathin bronchoscopy offers 
a feasible, safe, and reproducible method for diagnosing cavitary 
lung lesions. Its versatility under both general anesthesia and 
moderate sedation supports broad clinical applicability. Further 
studies should validate its diagnostic yield, safety profile, and costeffectiveness in larger patient cohorts.157

In cases combining both modalities, the robotic catheter 
was first advanced to the lesion, and standard tissue 
sampling was performed under fluoroscopic guidance. 
Following biopsy completion, the catheter was withdrawn, 
and the ultrathin bronchoscope was introduced along the 
same navigational pathway. The ultrathin bronchoscope 
enabled direct visualization of the lesion interior and 
facilitated selective or extensive targeted biopsies along 
the cavity wall with high precision. Its working channel 
accommodated small biopsy forceps and cryoprobes for 
tissue acquisition, as well as intracavitary fluid aspiration 
when indicated. Procedural workflow is shown in Figure 1 .
Procedural success was defined as successful advancement 
of the ultrathin bronchoscope into the target cavity under navigational guidance from the Intuitive PlanPoint software. 
Furthermore, enhanced diagnostic yield was defined as the 
acquisition of additional diagnostic information obtained 
with the ultrathin bronchoscope following robotic-guided 
biopsies, when applicable. This combined workflow was 
exclusively utilized in the four cases performed under GA.
Follow-up was performed 3 months after the procedure 
to assess radiographic and symptomatic improvement.

Discussion
Given the broad differential diagnosis of cavitary lung lesions, establishing an accurate diagnosis is essential (2). 
While radiographic features can offer valuable diagnostic 
clues, they are rarely definitive (1). Findings such as the halo 
sign, reversed halo sign, and variable cavitation patterns 
may help narrow the differential; however, tissue sampling 
remains indispensable for confirmation. Without biopsy, 
malignant disease may be missed or its diagnosis delayed, 
potentially leading to significant clinical consequences (3).

Flexible bronchoscopy capabilities have advanced 
considerably, with the development of thin and ultrathin 
instruments. T ypically defined as bronchoscopes with outer 
diameters of less than 4.0 and 3.5 mm, respectively, these 
devices enable deeper navigation within the bronchial tree, 
reaching up to the ninth generation of airways or beyond (6). 
Their reduced caliber facilitates access to peripheral nodules 
and the interior of cavitary lesions. When combined with 
adjunctive technologies such as fluoroscopy or radial 
endobronchial ultrasound (r-EBUS), their diagnostic yield is further enhanced (7). The ultrathin bronchoscope, in 
particular, with its narrower working channel, allows for 
precise, site-specific sampling within cavitary lesions and 
has demonstrated additional diagnostic value (7).
Despite these technological advances, several challenges 
persist in the biopsy of cavitary lung lesions. Because they 
are rarely homogeneous, the disease of interest may not 
be evenly distributed across the cavity wall. Necrotic or 
liquefied centers often fail to provide diagnostic material, 
resulting in nondiagnostic biopsies even when targeting is 
precise. Robotic systems also present unique challenges. 
The Ion platform (Intuitive), while offering the smallest 
catheter size in the market at 3.5 mm, does not provide 
direct visualization during biopsy, requiring reliance on 
fluoroscopy. Other platforms, such as the Galaxy (Noah 
Medical, San Carlos, USA) and Monarch (Johnson &

In this manuscript, we describe a workflow that integrates 
robotic and ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy within a single 
procedural session, designed to maximize diagnostic yield 
in cavitary lung lesions. The rationale for this approach 
lies in leveraging the roadmap created by navigational 
platforms to access the lesion and then employing ultrathin 
bronchoscopy to visualize the cavity directly and obtain 
targeted biopsies. By doing so, the operator benefits from 
both precise trajectory guidance and direct visualization, 
thereby overcoming limitations of each modality in isolation 
with a minimalistic tool footprint. 
The potential advantages of this combined approach 
are several. Diagnostic yield is optimized by combining 
precise navigational targeting with the ability to visualize 
and selectively sample viable tissue. Radiation exposure 
is reduced because reliance on fluoroscopy is diminished. 
The heterogeneity of cavitary lesions can be addressed by 
directly targeting different areas within the same cavity, 
improving the likelihood of identifying pathology. Direct 
visualization enhances safety by allowing operators to 
see the site of biopsy, reducing the risk of inadvertent 
injury. The flexibility of tool selection under direct vision 
further broadens sampling options (forceps, cryoprobe, 
etc.). Importantly, this workflow integrates seamlessly into 
routine practice without requiring additional equipment or 
major changes to procedural logistics.
Another important advantage of this approach is 
its adaptability to different clinical environments. In 
settings where GA is not available, the roadmap created 
by the navigational platform can be used with flexible 
bronchoscopy under moderate sedation or topical 
anesthesia. This feature may be particularly relevant in 
resource-limited settings, where anesthetic support is 
restricted.
These promising outcomes suggest that the method is 
both feasible and safe. Nonetheless, we acknowledge that 
this represents a preliminary experience, and larger studies 
are warranted to further validate these findings.
Conclusions
Cavitary lung lesions are diagnostically challenging because 
of their broad differential diagnosis, heterogeneous 
internal architecture, and elevated risk of complications 
during biopsy. Conventional approaches such as CTTB and surgical biopsy carry higher complication rates, while 
bronchoscopic modalities, though safer, may be limited by 
limited direct visualization or suboptimal yield.
The combination of navigational robotic bronchoscopy 
with ultrathin flexible bronchoscopy provides a practical 
solution by integrating the strengths of both modalities. 
Navigation establishes a precise roadmap and trajectory, 
while ultrathin bronchoscopy enables direct visualization 
and targeted sampling of viable tissue. T ogether, they create 
a synergistic workflow that could enhance diagnostic yield, 
reduces reliance on fluoroscopy, and integrates easily into 
existing procedural routines.
Our early institutional experience demonstrates the 
feasibility of this method, with successful outcomes in 
consecutive cases. These findings provide a foundation for 
broader clinical validation and integration. Future studies 
are warranted to formally evaluate diagnostic yield, safety, 
and cost-effectiveness across larger patient cohorts.
